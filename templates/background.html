That is the core problem. A "one-size-fits-all" dose for a drug like Warfarin is a dangerous guessing game. Every patient's body is different, and a standard dose can lead to two critical errors:
1.Under-dosing: The patient isn't protected and could suffer a stroke or blood clot.

1.The Enhanced Model (Clinical + Genome): This is our best and most accurate model. It's used when the hospital has access to advanced genomic testing (the patient's CYP2C9 and VKORC1 gene data).
2.The Base Model (Clinical-Only): Genomic tests are expensive and can take time. In many clinics, this data isn't available. Our "Base Model" is designed for this scenario. It provides a robust prediction using only standard clinical data like age and weight.
Our system's "Mode Selector" automatically detects if genomic data is provided. This way, it always uses the best possible model for the situation, making our tool adaptable to any hospital, not just advanced research labs.

This is the most important feature for building trust. A doctor will never trust a "black box" that just gives a number. For a doctor to be responsible for a patient, they need to know why the AI is making a recommendation.
1.What it is: We integrated a library called SHAP. For every single prediction, SHAP shows us the exact impact of each of the patient's features.
2.How it works: Our report generates a chart and simple sentences like: "The patient's high weight was a major factor, significantly decreasing the required dose," or "The VKORC1 gene was a factor, moderately increasing the required dose."
3.The Result: This gives the doctor full transparency. They can see the AI's "reasoning," and it becomes a trustworthy co-pilot, not a mysterious black box.

We started with a simple baseline model (Linear Regression), but the problem is too complex for a simple formula. We chose a Random Forest for three specific reasons:
1.High Accuracy: It's an "ensemble" model, which means it works like a "committee of 100 experts" (decision trees) who vote on the best answer. This "wisdom of the crowd" approach is extremely accurate at finding complex, non-linear patterns in biological data.
2.Explainability: It works perfectly with the SHAP library, allowing us to build our "Explainability" feature.
3.Confidence Score: Because it's a committee, we can measure how much the 100 "experts" agree. We built a "Prediction Confidence Score" from this. If all 100 trees give a similar prediction, the confidence is "High." If they disagree, the confidence is "Low," which tells the doctor to use extra caution.

This is a great question and it highlights the difference between accuracy and confidence.
1.Accuracy (RMSE): This is our overall score, which is very high. It's like a student's final grade of 98%. It proves the model is excellent on average.
2.Confidence: This is a score for a single prediction. A "Low" confidence score is one of the most valuable features of our project. It means the model is saying: "This specific patient is an unusual or 'edge' case. I have not seen many patients with this exact combination of factors. My best guess is 25.1 mg, but you should be cautious."
3.A simple model gives a wrong answer and acts 100% certain. Our advanced model gives a good prediction and has the intelligence to tell the doctor when to be careful. It's a safety feature that makes our tool truly responsible for clinical use.
